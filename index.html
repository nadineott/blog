MakerSquare: Week 1 Day 3 10:13pm
First self assessment was today and man was I nervous. It went as expected- it was chaotic and I felt rushed and panicked and nervous and terrified the whole time. I couldn’t be happier. Not because it went well, it didn’t, but because I survived and now I know I can survive whatever else comes my way. Bring it.

Something that struck me today was how I hadn’t had any coffee at all, was functioning on 4 hours of sleep, and didn’t feel tired. Let me elucidate on this. I am not a person who has ever been able to function well on little sleep, in fact in my experience I function quite poorly on anything less than 7 hours. I usually throw back coffee to no avail and find myself daydreaming of a pristine bed magically appearing for me to collapse into, while my eyelids grow heavy and I am unable to focus on the task at hand. Weirdly enough this hasn’t been the case since I stated MakerSquare. I think it’s because I am constantly stimulated and interested in the task at hand. Give my brain something to focus on besides the exhaustion hanging heavy like a thick fog and the fog ceases to exist! Even weirder than this new phenomenon is that instead of being pumped about it, I’m a bit saddened by how it took me this long to discover it. Oh the perspective I’m gaining after only three days, I wonder what I’ll learn tomorrow.

How it started
Not long ago I found myself talking to my superior at work regarding a problem that, given an engineering fix, would have saved me about 30 minutes per day. Let me be clear- this was 30 minutes of entirely pointless transferring data from point A to point B. I was on a team of ten people, and assuming we all got hit with this issue equally, that would be about 5 hours per day, 25 hours per week, 100 hours per month. 100 hours a month to be used for far better puroses after this engineering fix, which apparently was a "week long fix" on the part of an engineer. I’ll equate that to 50 hours of work.

Bear with me here while I do some simple math. My team spent 100 hours per month doing this. It would have taken an engineer 50 hours to eliminate this process for my team completely. How great is that? Two weeks after implementing this fix the company would have broken even on employee time used vs. employee time gained. What I didn’t understand was that my math was wrong. An hour of my time was not worth an hour of an engineer’s time. Not even close.

When I proposed we move forward at full speed with the fix, I was told that it was "not worth it." Mind. Blown. 100 hours of my teams time per month on an ongoing basis is "not worth" 50 hours of an engineer’s time.

Have you ever been picked last for kickball? Or dumped out of nowhere by someone who you thought was pretty swell? It felt kind of like that. I wanted to jump up and down and scream "I’m a worthwhile person! I work my butt off and I’m pretty smart to boot! Why is my time worth nothing compared to that person’s time?" Instead, I went with the smile, nod, and the ever fitting "Thanks for taking the time to look into it."

I thought about figuring out a way to bring this issue to my superiors in a new light, add more value to the proposition, you know the drill. But this hadn’t been the first time I had been stonewalled with the ever powerful "engineering effort" argument. I had a feeling it wouldn’t be the last. That night when I got home I Googled "learn to code."

And that’s how it started.
